FAQS

  • Q1. Am I too old to learn to surf?
  • If you're fit to swim you can learn to surf.
  • Q2. Am I too heavy to surf?
  • There are boards available to accomodate all shapes and sizes, the correct board length and volume are key to buoyancy for all surfers.
  • Q3. How do I get really good at surfing fast?
  • Spending as much time in the water as possible and not transiting from large boards to smaller boards too quickly, vital lessons will be missed.
  • Q4. Who is the best surfer of all time?
  • Kelly Slater (11 world titles).
  • Q5. Should I buy a new board?
  • Unless you have substantial means, rent 'FOAMIES' until you get more confident or buy second hand boards. Talking to an experienced surf shop assistant is a good idea at this stage. If you are lucky they will give you some tips!
  • Q6. What wave height should beginners surf?
  • Generally, 1.5ft to 2ft.

TYPES OF WAVES

  • What is a wave?
  • Waves are made when energy passes through water. They get their source of energy from the winds force on the oceans surface. The bigger ones are usually produced offshore, the smaller ones are created closer to shore.
  • Surging waves
  • Bad conditions for surfing, this type of wave comes out of deep water onto steep beaches and rather than breaking properly it will surge up the beach.
  • Spilling waves
  • Produced by a gently sloping seafloor, it casuses the wave to gradually peak. Due to the energy release being lazy, the crest 'spills' down the face of the wave.
  • Plunging waves
  • The best waves for surfing, formed when a swell transitions from deep oceean to very shallow water, like a reef. It obstruct's the waves forward velocity and causes the right kind of crest surfers love.

TYPES OF BREAKS

Breaks determine how the energy of a swell will be released when it hits the shoreline.

  • Beach breaks - wave moving from deep to shallow water over a sandy seafloor (Better for novices, less energy, soft landing for falls)
  • Reef breaks - waves breaking over a rocky bottom producing great waves (Better for experienced surfers, high risk of injury from falls)
  • Point breaks - wave breaking perpendicular to land, results in waves 'peeling' breaking perfectly for longer periods (Advanced surfers)

TYPES OF BOARDS

  • FOAMIE
  • Length 6' - 8', high volume, soft exterior (Beginner surfers)
  • FUNBOARD
  • Length 6' - 8', pretty much a fiber glass equivalent of a foamie
  • SHORTBOARD
  • Range in length form 5' - 7'. Have 2 to 4 fins to allow for fast turns. Have an upturned rocker (nose rocker) to help the user avoid nose diving into the water. (Advanced surfers)
  • BIG WAVE GUN
  • Specifically used for big waves and nothing else. As the name suggests they are bigger, 8' - 9' and possess a higher volume (higher buoyancy) to accomodate for the big wave intensity (Advanced surfers).
  • LONGBOARD
  • Length 8' - 10'6", good for beginners, high volume, easier to paddle and take off (All surfers)
  • FISH
  • Shorter and wider than short boards, very agile in small surfing conditions, go fast in slow breaking waves (Intermediate and up surfers)
